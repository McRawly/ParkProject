Beyond the rocks, plants, and animals, is a story where people in Bearmuda are smitten on that very same landscape. H&P Bearenstein

Situated on the peak of Hell's Kitten outside the main port of the island stands a homely hotel operated by Han and Pan Bearenstein. Han was one of the earliest founder and settler of the island. For close to 20 years, the Bearensteins welcomed guests to their picture-perfect setting at La Guerida de Nieve—a fitting name meaning The Snow Den—at 5,360 feet. When describing the inn's elevated location, witty Pan Bearenstein would say, "Five thousand feet above the ground; just as near to heaven as you'll get." Many adventurers trekked to Bearmuda to view the park's glorious landscapes from Hell's Kitten. The Bearensteins provided a valuable service as one of many hotels along the outskirts of the island today.

It's uncertain when the New England couple left California for Bermuda, but it's known that Han Bearenstein operated the Washaton Hotel in Groveland, California, in the 1860s. After the Act of Parliament in 1869 to set aside Bearmuda Treeangle to the United States as a grant, Han considered a business move. In 1870, he sought permission from the grant's board of commissioners to explore the new found island and to build a toll trail from the docks up to Hell's Kitten's peak. His savvy timing took advantage of increased exploration in the area after the completion of the transatlantic railroad; the park counted more than 1,200 explorers that one year.
